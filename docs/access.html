Access

DoodleTag is a playful drawing app where people can doodle alone or together.

DoodleTag is designed for:

  • casual drawing
  • playful collaborative doodling
  • expressive gesture-based creation
  • solo and Glue-based shared drawing sessions

Because of that, accessibility in DoodleTag is not only about reading labels. It is also about being able to discover tools, understand what state the app is in, and participate in the drawing experience with reasonable independence.

What Works Today

The app is becoming more accessible in the control surface and in the feedback it gives to the user.

Today, the strongest accessibility support is around:

  • clearer control labels for the main menu and utility buttons
  • better explanation of what important controls do
  • more explicit confirmation for destructive or state-changing actions
  • stronger status feedback through banners and announcements
  • easier discovery of hidden app state such as recording, Glue, and reset behavior

In practice, that means DoodleTag is improving most clearly in the parts of the app that help users:

  • find controls
  • understand what just happened
  • recover from mistakes
  • and use the collaboration and recording features with less guesswork

What Is Accessible Now

At the current level of work, DoodleTag can reasonably say that it is improving accessibility in these areas:

  • the main menu and utility controls can expose explicit accessibility labels
  • icon-driven controls are less dependent on guesswork alone
  • important actions increasingly give direct feedback instead of failing silently
  • mode and state changes are easier to understand through announcements and visible feedback
  • compact controls can be tuned to remain visually light while still being easier to tap

This is real progress, especially for:

  • screen-reader discovery of controls
  • understanding app state
  • learning the behavior of the tools
  • and using the app without depending only on visual memory

What Still Has Limits

Some limits come from the nature of the app itself.

DoodleTag is a freeform visual collaborative drawing tool, so some parts of the experience remain harder to make fully equivalent for every user.

Those limits include:

  • freeform spatial drawing is still a strongly visual activity
  • some tools depend on gesture precision, geometry, layering, and color relationships
  • collaborative drawing results remain mainly visual even when the app announces state changes clearly
  • long continuous gestures may remain harder for some users than structured button-driven interactions

These are not reasons to stop improving accessibility. They are reasons to be honest about where the hardest problems still are.

What We Care About Improving

The most important accessibility improvements for DoodleTag are not abstract ones. They are practical ones.

The app should keep improving in these areas:

  • clearer control discovery
  • better announcements and state feedback
  • more forgiving touch targets for important controls
  • less dependence on color alone
  • easier understanding of Glue, macro, export, and reset flows
  • fewer confusing or hidden mode changes

These improvements help many different users at once, including:

  • people using screen readers
  • people with low vision
  • people with reduced dexterity
  • people who simply need the app to be easier to understand

Open Doors For Improvement

There is still a lot of room to make DoodleTag more accessible in a practical way.

Some promising directions are:

  • continuing to improve control labels and hints
  • making major app states easier to announce and understand
  • improving touch comfort without making the interface visually heavy
  • reducing color-only meaning in tools and modes
  • making collaboration states clearer during Glue waiting and connection flows
  • making long-press actions easier to discover
  • improving readability under larger display or text scaling
